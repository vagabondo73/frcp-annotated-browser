Landmark Cases in U.S. Civil Procedure

Personal Jurisdiction (7)

Pennoyer v. Neff, 95 U.S. 714 (1878)

Holding: A state court's in personam jurisdiction requires either personal service within the state or the defendant's voluntary appearance. State authority is strictly territorial.

Significance: Foundational territorial model of personal jurisdiction, later transformed by International Shoe.

Provisions: Fourteenth Amendment — Due Process & Equal Protection · Rule 4. Summons

International Shoe Co. v. Washington, 326 U.S. 310 (1945)

Holding: Due process requires that a defendant have 'minimum contacts' with the forum state such that maintenance of the suit does not offend 'traditional notions of fair play and substantial justice.'

Significance: Replaced Pennoyer's strict territorial model with the minimum contacts framework that governs personal jurisdiction today.

Provisions: Fourteenth Amendment — Due Process & Equal Protection · Rule 4. Summons · Rule 12. Defenses and Objections: When and How Presented; Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings; Consolidating Motions; Waiving Defenses; Pretrial Hearing

📄 Full Text (Justia) · CourtListener · Google Scholar

Burger King Corp. v. Rudzewicz, 471 U.S. 462 (1985)

Holding: Specific jurisdiction exists where a defendant has 'purposefully directed' activities at residents of the forum state and the litigation arises from injuries relating to those activities.

Significance: Elaborated the 'purposeful availment' and 'purposeful direction' tests for specific jurisdiction.

Provisions: Fourteenth Amendment — Due Process & Equal Protection · Rule 4. Summons

📄 Full Text (Justia) · CourtListener · Google Scholar

Asahi Metal Industry Co. v. Superior Court, 480 U.S. 102 (1987)

Holding: Placing goods into the stream of commerce, without more, may be insufficient for specific jurisdiction. The 'reasonableness' inquiry is an independent check on jurisdiction.

Significance: Created the stream-of-commerce split between the O'Connor ('plus') and Brennan ('awareness') tests.

Provisions: Fourteenth Amendment — Due Process & Equal Protection · Rule 4. Summons

📄 Full Text (Justia) · CourtListener · Google Scholar

Bristol-Myers Squibb Co. v. Superior Court, 582 U.S. 255 (2017)

Holding: In mass-tort litigation, nonresident plaintiffs' claims must have an adequate connection to the forum state; they cannot 'ride' on resident plaintiffs' contacts.

Significance: Tightened specific jurisdiction requirements in mass-tort/multi-plaintiff litigation.

Provisions: Fourteenth Amendment — Due Process & Equal Protection · Rule 4. Summons

📄 Full Text (Justia) · Google Scholar

Ford Motor Co. v. Montana Eighth Judicial District Court, 592 U.S. 351 (2021)

Holding: Specific jurisdiction does not require a strict causal link between the defendant's forum contacts and the plaintiff's claim; a sufficient 'connection' or 'relationship' suffices.

Significance: Rejected a rigid causation requirement, holding that Ford's extensive activities in the forum related to the vehicle models at issue.

Provisions: Fourteenth Amendment — Due Process & Equal Protection · Rule 4. Summons

📄 Full Text (Justia) · Google Scholar

Walden v. Fiore, 571 U.S. 277 (2014)

Holding: The minimum-contacts analysis looks to the defendant's contacts with the forum state itself, not the defendant's contacts with persons who reside there.

Significance: Confirmed defendant-focused nature of the jurisdictional inquiry.

Provisions: Fourteenth Amendment — Due Process & Equal Protection · Rule 4. Summons

Standing (3)

Lujan v. Defenders of Wildlife, 504 U.S. 555 (1992)

Holding: Article III standing requires (1) injury in fact, (2) causation, and (3) redressability. The plaintiff must have suffered a concrete and particularized injury.

Significance: Canonical three-part standing test; foundational case for justiciability.

Provisions: Article III — Judicial Power · Rule 12. Defenses and Objections: When and How Presented; Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings; Consolidating Motions; Waiving Defenses; Pretrial Hearing

📄 Full Text (Justia) · CourtListener · Google Scholar

Spokeo, Inc. v. Robins, 578 U.S. 330 (2016)

Holding: Article III standing requires a 'concrete' injury even for statutory violations; a bare procedural violation may not suffice without actual harm.

Significance: Limited standing for statutory damages claims without concrete harm.

Provisions: Article III — Judicial Power

📄 Full Text (Justia) · Google Scholar

TransUnion LLC v. Ramirez, 594 U.S. 413 (2021)

Holding: Plaintiffs in federal court must demonstrate a concrete harm to have Article III standing, even in a class action alleging statutory violations. Only class members whose inaccurate information was disseminated to third parties suffered concrete harm.

Significance: Further tightened the concreteness requirement for statutory-damages classes.

Provisions: Article III — Judicial Power · Rule 23. Class Actions

Subject-Matter Jurisdiction (4)

Louisville & Nashville Railroad Co. v. Mottley, 211 U.S. 149 (1908)

Holding: Federal-question jurisdiction under 28 U.S.C. § 1331 requires that the federal question appear on the face of a well-pleaded complaint; a federal defense is insufficient.

Significance: Established the well-pleaded complaint rule for federal-question jurisdiction.

Provisions: 28 U.S.C. § 1331 — Federal Question Jurisdiction · Rule 12. Defenses and Objections: When and How Presented; Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings; Consolidating Motions; Waiving Defenses; Pretrial Hearing

📄 Full Text (Justia) · Google Scholar

Strawbridge v. Curtiss, 7 U.S. (3 Cranch) 267 (1806)

Holding: Complete diversity is required: every plaintiff must be diverse from every defendant for diversity jurisdiction under § 1332.

Significance: Longstanding interpretation requiring complete diversity, never overruled.

Provisions: 28 U.S.C. § 1332 — Diversity Jurisdiction

📄 Full Text (Justia) · Google Scholar

Exxon Mobil Corp. v. Allapattah Services, Inc., 545 U.S. 546 (2005)

Holding: Under 28 U.S.C. § 1367, supplemental jurisdiction allows a federal court to exercise jurisdiction over additional claims that do not independently meet the amount-in-controversy requirement, so long as at least one plaintiff satisfies it.

Significance: Resolved circuit split on supplemental jurisdiction over claims below the amount in controversy.

Provisions: 28 U.S.C. § 1332 — Diversity Jurisdiction · 28 U.S.C. § 1367 — Supplemental Jurisdiction

📄 Full Text (Justia) · Google Scholar

Grable & Sons Metal Products, Inc. v. Darue Engineering & Manufacturing, 545 U.S. 308 (2005)

Holding: Federal-question jurisdiction over state-law claims exists when the state-law claim necessarily raises a disputed and substantial federal issue that a federal forum may entertain without disturbing the congressionally approved balance.

Significance: Refined the 'embedded federal question' doctrine beyond the well-pleaded complaint rule.

Provisions: 28 U.S.C. § 1331 — Federal Question Jurisdiction

Erie Doctrine (6)

Erie Railroad Co. v. Tompkins, 304 U.S. 64 (1938)

Holding: There is no federal general common law. Except in matters governed by the Federal Constitution or by Acts of Congress, the law to be applied in any case is the law of the state.

Significance: Overruled Swift v. Tyson; most important federalism decision in civil procedure.

Provisions: 28 U.S.C. § 1652 — State Laws as Rules of Decision (Rules of Decision Act) · Article III — Judicial Power

📄 Full Text (Justia) · CourtListener · Google Scholar

Guaranty Trust Co. v. York, 326 U.S. 99 (1945)

Holding: In diversity cases, if applying the federal rule would 'significantly affect the result,' the state rule must apply (outcome-determinative test).

Significance: Established the 'outcome determination' test as the Erie standard.

Provisions: 28 U.S.C. § 1652 — State Laws as Rules of Decision (Rules of Decision Act)

📄 Full Text (Justia) · CourtListener · Google Scholar

Byrd v. Blue Ridge Rural Electric Cooperative, 356 U.S. 525 (1958)

Holding: In determining whether to apply state or federal practice, courts must weigh 'countervailing considerations' of the federal system, including the Seventh Amendment right to jury trial.

Significance: Introduced interest-balancing into the Erie analysis.

Provisions: 28 U.S.C. § 1652 — State Laws as Rules of Decision (Rules of Decision Act) · Seventh Amendment — Right to Jury Trial

📄 Full Text (Justia) · CourtListener · Google Scholar

Hanna v. Plumer, 380 U.S. 460 (1965)

Holding: When a Federal Rule of Civil Procedure directly governs the issue, it applies if it is valid under the Rules Enabling Act. The 'twin aims of Erie' — discouragement of forum shopping and avoidance of inequitable administration of the laws — guide only 'unguided Erie choices.'

Significance: Created the two-track Erie framework: REA track for FRCP rules, modified outcome-determination for judge-made rules.

Provisions: 28 U.S.C. § 1652 — State Laws as Rules of Decision (Rules of Decision Act) · 28 U.S.C. § 2072 — Rules Enabling Act · Rule 4. Summons

📄 Full Text (Justia) · CourtListener · Google Scholar

Gasperini v. Center for Humanities, Inc., 518 U.S. 415 (1996)

Holding: A state law standard for reviewing the adequacy of jury verdicts can be accommodated within the federal system by applying it at the trial court level rather than the appellate level.

Significance: Demonstrated accommodation between state substantive standards and federal procedural interests.

Provisions: 28 U.S.C. § 1652 — State Laws as Rules of Decision (Rules of Decision Act) · Seventh Amendment — Right to Jury Trial

📄 Full Text (Justia) · Google Scholar

Semtek International Inc. v. Lockheed Martin Corp., 531 U.S. 497 (2001)

Holding: The claim-preclusive effect of a federal diversity judgment is determined by federal common law, which will usually adopt the preclusion law of the state in which the rendering court sits.

Significance: Applied Erie principles to the preclusive effect of federal diversity judgments.

Provisions: 28 U.S.C. § 1652 — State Laws as Rules of Decision (Rules of Decision Act) · Rule 41. Dismissal of Actions

Pleading (5)

Conley v. Gibson, 355 U.S. 41 (1957)

Holding: A complaint should not be dismissed for failure to state a claim unless it appears beyond doubt that the plaintiff can prove no set of facts in support of his claim.

Significance: Established the liberal 'no set of facts' standard that governed pleading for 50 years (overruled by Twombly).

Provisions: Rule 8. General Rules of Pleading · Rule 12. Defenses and Objections: When and How Presented; Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings; Consolidating Motions; Waiving Defenses; Pretrial Hearing

📄 Full Text (Justia) · CourtListener · Google Scholar

Bell Atlantic Corp. v. Twombly, 550 U.S. 544 (2007)

Holding: To survive a motion to dismiss under Rule 12(b)(6), a complaint must state enough facts to state a claim to relief that is 'plausible on its face,' not merely conceivable. Retired the Conley 'no set of facts' standard.

Significance: Replaced Conley with plausibility pleading, the most significant change to pleading standards in modern procedure.

Provisions: Rule 8. General Rules of Pleading · Rule 12. Defenses and Objections: When and How Presented; Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings; Consolidating Motions; Waiving Defenses; Pretrial Hearing

📄 Full Text (Justia) · CourtListener · Google Scholar

Ashcroft v. Iqbal, 556 U.S. 662 (2009)

Holding: The Twombly plausibility standard applies to all civil actions, not just antitrust cases. Courts should (1) identify conclusory allegations that are not entitled to the assumption of truth and (2) determine whether remaining factual allegations plausibly state a claim.

Significance: Extended Twombly's plausibility standard to all federal civil litigation and added the two-step methodology.

Provisions: Rule 8. General Rules of Pleading · Rule 12. Defenses and Objections: When and How Presented; Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings; Consolidating Motions; Waiving Defenses; Pretrial Hearing

📄 Full Text (Justia) · CourtListener · Google Scholar

Leatherman v. Tarrant County Narcotics Intelligence & Coordination Unit, 507 U.S. 163 (1993)

Holding: Federal courts may not impose a heightened pleading standard in civil rights cases beyond what is required by Rules 8 and 9.

Significance: Rejected judicial adoption of heightened pleading; only Rule 9(b) requires specificity.

Provisions: Rule 8. General Rules of Pleading · Rule 9. Pleading Special Matters

📄 Full Text (Justia) · Google Scholar

Swierkiewicz v. Sorema N.A., 534 U.S. 506 (2002)

Holding: An employment discrimination plaintiff need not plead specific facts establishing a prima facie case under the McDonnell Douglas framework; the ordinary Rule 8(a)(2) notice pleading standard applies.

Significance: Reinforced liberal notice pleading pre-Twombly; its vitality post-Iqbal is debated.

Provisions: Rule 8. General Rules of Pleading

Discovery (3)

Hickman v. Taylor, 329 U.S. 495 (1947)

Holding: An attorney's work product — written statements, mental impressions, legal theories prepared in anticipation of litigation — is qualifiedly protected from discovery.

Significance: Created the work-product doctrine, later codified in Rule 26(b)(3).

Provisions: Rule 26. Duty to Disclose; General Provisions Governing Discovery

📄 Full Text (Justia) · CourtListener · Google Scholar

Upjohn Co. v. United States, 449 U.S. 383 (1981)

Holding: The attorney-client privilege in the corporate context extends to communications between corporate counsel and lower-level employees, rejecting the 'control group' test.

Significance: Broadened corporate attorney-client privilege beyond the 'control group.'

Provisions: FRE 501. Privilege in General · Rule 26. Duty to Disclose; General Provisions Governing Discovery

📄 Full Text (Justia) · CourtListener · Google Scholar

Seattle Times Co. v. Rhinehart, 467 U.S. 20 (1984)

Holding: A protective order restricting dissemination of discovered information does not offend the First Amendment where the order is entered on a showing of good cause under Rule 26(c).

Significance: Established that discovery material is not public and may be restricted without First Amendment violation.

Provisions: Rule 26. Duty to Disclose; General Provisions Governing Discovery

Summary Judgment (3)

Celotex Corp. v. Catrett, 477 U.S. 317 (1986)

Holding: A movant for summary judgment need not produce evidence negating the nonmovant's claim; the movant may meet its burden by demonstrating the absence of evidence supporting the nonmovant's case.

Significance: One of the 1986 trilogy; liberalized the movant's initial burden on summary judgment.

Provisions: Rule 56. Summary Judgment

📄 Full Text (Justia) · CourtListener · Google Scholar

Anderson v. Liberty Lobby, Inc., 477 U.S. 242 (1986)

Holding: The substantive evidentiary standard of proof (e.g., clear and convincing evidence) applies at the summary judgment stage when evaluating whether a genuine issue of material fact exists.

Significance: One of the 1986 trilogy; incorporated the evidentiary standard into the Rule 56 analysis.

Provisions: Rule 56. Summary Judgment

📄 Full Text (Justia) · CourtListener · Google Scholar

Matsushita Electric Industrial Co. v. Zenith Radio Corp., 475 U.S. 574 (1986)

Holding: Where the nonmoving party's evidence is merely colorable or not significantly probative, summary judgment may be granted. In antitrust conspiracy cases, the nonmovant must show evidence that tends to exclude the possibility of independent action.

Significance: One of the 1986 trilogy; raised the bar for the nonmovant's showing on summary judgment.

Provisions: Rule 56. Summary Judgment

Class Actions (4)

Hansberry v. Lee, 311 U.S. 32 (1940)

Holding: A class action judgment binds absent class members only if there was adequate representation of their interests; otherwise, the judgment violates due process.

Significance: Constitutional foundation for class action adequacy of representation.

Provisions: Fourteenth Amendment — Due Process & Equal Protection · Rule 23. Class Actions

📄 Full Text (Justia) · CourtListener · Google Scholar

Wal-Mart Stores, Inc. v. Dukes, 564 U.S. 338 (2011)

Holding: Rule 23(a)(2) commonality requires that class members' claims depend on a common contention whose truth or falsity will resolve an issue that is central to the validity of each claim in one stroke. The plaintiffs failed to identify a common policy of discrimination.

Significance: Raised the bar for commonality and questioned (b)(2) certification for monetary claims.

Provisions: Rule 23. Class Actions

📄 Full Text (Justia) · Google Scholar

Comcast Corp. v. Behrend, 569 U.S. 27 (2013)

Holding: At the class certification stage under Rule 23(b)(3), plaintiffs must demonstrate that damages are capable of measurement on a classwide basis using a model consistent with the theory of liability.

Significance: Required alignment between the theory of liability and the damages model at certification.

Provisions: Rule 23. Class Actions

📄 Full Text (Justia) · Google Scholar

Shady Grove Orthopedic Associates, P.A. v. Allstate Insurance Co., 559 U.S. 393 (2010)

Holding: Rule 23 governs whether a case may proceed as a class action in federal court, even if a state statute would prohibit class treatment. Rule 23 is valid under the Rules Enabling Act.

Significance: Major Erie/class-action intersection; confirmed FRCP 23 preempts contrary state class-action restrictions in federal court.

Provisions: Rule 23. Class Actions · 28 U.S.C. § 2072 — Rules Enabling Act

Preclusion (2)

Parklane Hosiery Co. v. Shore, 439 U.S. 322 (1979)

Holding: Offensive nonmutual collateral estoppel may be applied at the discretion of the trial court, considering fairness factors including whether the plaintiff could easily have joined the prior action.

Significance: Approved offensive nonmutual issue preclusion, expanding the reach of prior judgments.

Provisions: Rule 8. General Rules of Pleading · Seventh Amendment — Right to Jury Trial

📄 Full Text (Justia) · CourtListener · Google Scholar

Taylor v. Sturgell, 553 U.S. 880 (2008)

Holding: Claim preclusion may not be imposed against a nonparty to the prior litigation except in six recognized categories (agreement, preclusion of class members, etc.). Rejected 'virtual representation' theory.

Significance: Narrowed and clarified the circumstances under which nonparties are bound by prior judgments.

Provisions: Fifth Amendment — Due Process

Jury Trial Right (3)

Beacon Theatres, Inc. v. Westover, 359 U.S. 500 (1959)

Holding: Where both legal and equitable claims are raised, the legal claims must be tried first to preserve the Seventh Amendment jury trial right. The expansion of Rule 2's merger of law and equity does not diminish the right to jury trial.

Significance: Established the priority of jury trial rights when legal and equitable claims overlap.

Provisions: Seventh Amendment — Right to Jury Trial · Rule 38. Right to a Jury Trial; Demand · Rule 39. Trial by Jury or by the Court

📄 Full Text (Justia) · CourtListener · Google Scholar

Dairy Queen, Inc. v. Wood, 369 U.S. 469 (1962)

Holding: Where legal issues are present in a case, the Seventh Amendment right to a jury trial on those issues is not lost merely because the case is styled as an equitable proceeding.

Significance: Extended Beacon Theatres; strengthened jury-trial rights in mixed cases.

Provisions: Seventh Amendment — Right to Jury Trial · Rule 38. Right to a Jury Trial; Demand

📄 Full Text (Justia) · CourtListener · Google Scholar

Markman v. Westview Instruments, Inc., 517 U.S. 370 (1996)

Holding: The construction of patent claims is a matter of law reserved for the court, not a factual question for the jury, consistent with the Seventh Amendment.

Significance: Major patent procedure case; illustrates the law/fact divide under the Seventh Amendment.

Provisions: Seventh Amendment — Right to Jury Trial

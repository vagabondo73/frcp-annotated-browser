Section I · Modules 1–5, 13–17

FRCP History & Structure

Origins of the 1938 Rules, the rulemaking process, and the foundational architecture of federal civil procedure.

Source 1
Stephen N. Subrin, How Equity Conquered Common Law: The Federal Rules of Civil Procedure in Historical Perspective
135 U. Pa. L. Rev. 909 (1987) · scholarship.law.upenn.edu

The 1938 Federal Rules of Civil Procedure represented the triumph of equity procedure over common law procedure. The Rules' drafters, led by Charles Clark, consciously chose the flexible, discretionary model of equity over the rigid, technical forms of the common law. This choice has had profound consequences for how we understand the purpose of procedure, the role of the judge, and the meaning of access to justice.

Related Cases
Hanna v. Plumer Shady Grove
Source 2
Federal Judicial Center, The Development of the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure: A Historical Overview
FJC Historical Research (ongoing) · fjc.gov

The Federal Rules of Civil Procedure, which became effective September 16, 1938, were the product of a decades-long reform effort. Charles Clark of Yale Law School served as Reporter for the Advisory Committee, and the Rules he helped draft replaced the Conformity Act's hodgepodge of state procedures with a uniform, trans-substantive code designed to secure the just, speedy, and inexpensive determination of every action.

Section II · Modules 14–17

Pleading Standards

The Conley → Twombly → Iqbal arc and comparative critiques of the plausibility regime.

Source 3
Arthur R. Miller, From Conley to Twombly to Iqbal: A Double Play on the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure
60 Duke L.J. 1 (2010) · scholarship.law.duke.edu

Iqbal and Twombly represent a fundamental change in American pleading doctrine. For fifty years after Conley v. Gibson, it was understood that Rule 8(a)(2) required only notice — a short and plain statement. The Court in Twombly and Iqbal effectively rewrote the Rule without the notice-and-comment rulemaking process required by the Rules Enabling Act, imposing a plausibility threshold that has systematically disadvantaged claimants, particularly in civil rights cases.

Primary Rules
Rule 8(a) Rule 12(b)(6)
Key Cases
Conley v. Gibson Twombly Iqbal Leatherman Swierkiewicz
Source 4
Arthur R. Miller, Simplified Pleading, Meaningful Days in Court, and Trials on the Merits: Reflections on the Deformation of Federal Procedure
88 N.Y.U. L. Rev. 286 (2013) · nyulawreview.org (direct PDF)

The original trans-substantive aspiration of the Federal Rules — that the same procedural framework would apply to every civil dispute regardless of subject matter — has been distorted by the Supreme Court's recent decisions. Twombly and Iqbal, combined with the tightening of summary judgment practice after the 1986 trilogy, have created a procedure that systematically filters out certain categories of claims before any discovery can take place.

Primary Rules
Rule 8(a) Rule 56
Key Cases
Twombly Iqbal Celotex
Source 5
Samuel Dodson, New Pleading, New Discovery
109 Mich. L. Rev. 53 (2010) · repository.law.umich.edu

Twombly and Iqbal did not merely raise the pleading bar — they changed the relationship between pleading and discovery. By requiring plaintiffs to allege plausible facts before discovery, the Court has created a Catch-22: a plaintiff needs discovery to plead facts, but must plead facts to get discovery. This inversion of the traditional sequence is particularly consequential in complex antitrust and civil rights cases where the relevant facts are largely in the defendant's possession.

Primary Rules
Rule 8(a) Rule 26
Key Cases
Twombly Iqbal
Source 6
James R. Maxeiner, Pleading and Access to Civil Procedure: Historical and Comparative Reflections on Iqbal and a Proposed New Federal Rule
114 Penn St. L. Rev. 1257 (2010) · pennstatelawreview.org (direct PDF)

When compared to the German Zivilprozessordnung or the French Code de procédure civile, the Iqbal plausibility standard is remarkable in how much it requires plaintiffs to know before they have had any opportunity to investigate. Civil law systems traditionally allow a complaint to be far more skeletal, placing the burden of developing facts on the court. The American plausibility standard, viewed comparatively, is an outlier.

Section III · Modules 6–9

Personal Jurisdiction

From International Shoe to Ford Motor: the tightening of minimum contacts doctrine.

Source 7
Linda Silberman & Nathan D. Yaffe (updated Adam Stein), Personal Jurisdiction and the Internet: Returning to First Principles
56 N.Y.U. J. Int'l L. & Pol. 371 (2024) · nyujilp.org (direct PDF)

The Supreme Court's decisions in Bristol-Myers Squibb and Ford Motor have significantly tightened the relatedness requirement for specific jurisdiction, moving away from a broad "but for" causation test toward an "arise out of or relate to" standard that demands a stronger nexus. These decisions, combined with Walden v. Fiore's defendant-focused framework, have reconfigured the personal jurisdiction landscape in ways that courts and practitioners are still working to understand.

Primary Rules
Rule 4(k) 14th Amend.
Key Cases
Int'l Shoe Walden v. Fiore Bristol-Myers Ford Motor
Source 8
Adam N. Steinman, Beyond Bristol-Myers: The Future of Main Street Litigation
97 N.Y.U. L. Rev. 902 (2022) · nyulawreview.org (direct PDF)

Bristol-Myers Squibb's restriction on aggregated litigation for non-resident plaintiffs has profound implications beyond mass torts. It affects multi-plaintiff employment cases, consumer class actions, and any scenario where plaintiffs from multiple states seek to aggregate claims against a defendant in a single forum. The decision has accelerated a fragmentation of mass litigation that threatens both efficiency and access to justice.

Rule 4(k)

Source 9
Linda J. Silberman, A New State Registration Act: Legislating a Longer Arm for Personal Jurisdiction
SSRN Working Paper (2020) · papers.ssrn.com Free SSRN account required to download PDF

After Daimler AG v. Bauman effectively eliminated general jurisdiction over corporations except in their state of incorporation or principal place of business, and after Bristol-Myers restricted specific jurisdiction, there is a growing jurisdictional gap for plaintiffs injured by corporate defendants. A new uniform state registration act — requiring corporations registered to do business in a state to consent to jurisdiction there — could fill this gap without constitutional difficulty.

Subject-Matter Jurisdiction

Standing, diversity, federal question, supplemental, and removal jurisdiction.

Source 10
C.B. Whittaker, The Invisible Circumstances of Notice: Due Process, Service, and Modern Procedure
101 N.C. L. Rev. 855 (2023) · scholarship.law.unc.edu (direct PDF)

The constitutional requirement of reasonably calculated notice established in Mullane v. Central Hanover Bank & Trust has proven remarkably durable, but its application to digital communications and social media service has exposed significant tensions in the doctrine. Courts are divided on whether electronic service meets the Mullane standard, and the question of what constitutes adequate notice in the digital age remains unresolved.

Section V · Modules 25–26

Erie Doctrine & Choice of Law

The two-track Hanna framework, the Erie/REA intersection, and comparative reflections on federal/state law allocation.

Source 11
Federal Judicial Center, Erie Railroad Co. v. Tompkins: A Historical Case Study
FJC Cases That Shaped the Federal Courts (2020) · fjc.gov

Erie Railroad Co. v. Tompkins (1938) overruled Swift v. Tyson (1842) and its ninety-six-year legacy of federal general common law. Justice Brandeis declared it unconstitutional for federal courts to declare general common law rules in diversity cases. The decision had immediate and enduring consequences for how federal courts in diversity cases handle choice-of-law questions, and its implications continue to be worked out today.

Primary Rules
§ 1652 RDA § 2072 REA
Key Cases
Erie R.R. Guaranty Trust Hanna v. Plumer
Source 12
Kevin M. Clermont, The Unseen Track of Erie Railroad: Why History and Jurisprudence Suggest a Different Model of Erie Analysis
SSRN Working Paper (2008), Cornell L. Fac. Scholarship · papers.ssrn.com Free SSRN account required to download PDF

The standard Erie analysis moves too quickly from Hanna's two-track framework to the conclusion that a directly applicable Federal Rule governs. There is an underexplored intermediate track — cases where a Federal Rule exists but is not directly on point, requiring a more nuanced inquiry into whether federal practice or state law should govern the specific question at issue. Erie's jurisprudential underpinnings counsel greater attention to this intermediate category.

Section VI · Modules 21–23

Discovery & Proportionality

Work product, privilege, the 2015 proportionality amendment, ESI, and the regulatory function of discovery.

Source 13
Diego A. Zambrano, Discovery as Regulation
119 Mich. L. Rev. 71 (2020) · law.stanford.edu (direct PDF)

Discovery functions not merely as an information-gathering mechanism but as a regulatory instrument. The threat of burdensome discovery induces settlement and changes primary behavior, functioning as a de facto penalty on defendants independent of the merits of the underlying claim. The 2015 proportionality amendment to Rule 26(b)(1) was designed to address this dynamic, but its implementation has been uneven across districts and subject matters.

Primary Rules
Rule 26(b)(1) Rule 37
Key Cases
Hickman v. Taylor Seattle Times
Source 14
Guidelines and Best Practices for Implementing the 2015 Discovery Amendments Concerning Proportionality (Third Edition, 2021)
Duke Law Center for Judicial Studies (2021) · papers.ssrn.com Free SSRN account required to download PDF

The proportionality standard in Rule 26(b)(1) asks courts and parties to calibrate discovery to the needs of the case by weighing six factors: the importance of the issues, the amount in controversy, the parties' relative access to relevant information, the parties' resources, the importance of the discovery to resolving the issues, and whether the burden or expense of the proposed discovery outweighs its likely benefit. These Guidelines provide practical guidance for courts and practitioners navigating this multi-factor analysis.

Section VII · Module 24

Case Management & Judicial Role

The transformation of the federal judge from passive arbiter to active manager under Rule 16.

Source 15
Judith Resnik, Managerial Judges
96 Harv. L. Rev. 374 (1982) · openyls.law.yale.edu (direct PDF)

Judges have become managers, and the transformation is profound. Under the 1983 amendments to Rule 16, judges are expected to take an active role in shaping litigation from its earliest stages, setting discovery schedules, encouraging settlement, and narrowing issues for trial. This shift from the adversarial model — in which the judge is a neutral umpire — to the managerial model raises fundamental questions about judicial impartiality, the appearance of bias, and the meaning of due process.

Section VIII · Module 28

Summary Judgment

The 1986 Celotex trilogy, the constitutionality of Rule 56, and the evolution of the JMOL standard.

Source 16
A. Simmons et al., The Celotex Trilogy Revisited: Summary Judgment After Twenty Years
1 FAMU L. Rev. 1 (2006) · commons.law.famu.edu

Twenty years after the Supreme Court decided Celotex, Anderson, and Matsushita in a single Term, it is possible to assess the trilogy's impact. The three cases together shifted the summary judgment calculus dramatically in favor of defendants, particularly in complex litigation. Celotex eliminated the movant's burden to negate the nonmovant's case; Anderson incorporated the trial standard of proof into the summary judgment inquiry; Matsushita introduced economic plausibility as a threshold for jury consideration.

Primary Rules
Rule 56
Key Cases
Celotex Anderson Matsushita
Source 17
Suja A. Thomas, Why Summary Judgment Is Unconstitutional
93 Va. L. Rev. 139 (2007) · virginialawreview.org · papers.ssrn.com Free SSRN account required to download PDF

The Seventh Amendment's Re-examination Clause provides that no fact tried by a jury shall be otherwise re-examined in any Court of the United States than according to the rules of the common law. Summary judgment, as interpreted since the 1986 trilogy, requires courts to assess the sufficiency of evidence — a function that the historical record shows was exclusively reserved to the jury at common law in 1791. The trilogy cases therefore constitute a violation of the Seventh Amendment.

Section IX · Module 29

Jury Trial Rights — Seventh Amendment

The historical test, the Beacon Theatres framework, and the legal/equitable divide.

Source 18
Suja A. Thomas, The Seventh Amendment, Modern Procedure, and the English Common Law
82 Wash. U. L. Rev. 687 (2004) · openscholarship.wustl.edu (direct PDF)

The Supreme Court has long held that the Seventh Amendment's scope must be determined by reference to the common law of England as it existed in 1791. But the Court's application of this historical test has been inconsistent and, at times, result-oriented. Beacon Theatres and Dairy Queen correctly gave priority to legal claims over equitable claims when the two overlap, but the Court has been less faithful to history in other contexts, such as patent claim construction in Markman.

Primary Rules
Rule 38 7th Amend.
Key Cases
Beacon Theatres Dairy Queen Markman
Source 19
U.S. Constitution Annotated — Seventh Amendment: Civil Jury Trial
Constitution Annotated (CONAN), 2022 ed., at Art. VII · govinfo.gov (direct PDF)

The Seventh Amendment's Preservation Clause protects the right to jury trial in suits at common law where the value in controversy exceeds twenty dollars. The Supreme Court has construed this clause to require courts to look to the nature of the claim and remedy at English common law in 1791 to determine whether the right attaches. In cases of novel claims, courts employ a functional analysis comparing the claim to the most analogous historical action.

Class Actions — Rule 23

Certification standards, settlement classes, CAFA, and the governance challenge.

Source 20
Samuel Issacharoff & George Loewenstein, Second Thoughts About Summary Judgment
100 Yale L.J. 73 (1990) · cmu.edu (direct PDF)

The standard economic account of litigation assumes that parties settle when the expected value of settlement exceeds the expected value of continued litigation. But this account ignores the ways in which procedural asymmetries — differential discovery costs, differential access to evidence, and differential ability to absorb litigation risk — distort the settlement calculus. Summary judgment, as applied after the 1986 trilogy, functions as a device that systematically advantages well-resourced defendants at the expense of less-resourced plaintiffs.

Primary Rules
Rule 56 Rule 23
Key Cases
Celotex Anderson
Source 21
Samuel Issacharoff, Governance and Legitimacy in the Law of Class Actions
1999 Sup. Ct. Rev. 337 · ir.lawnet.fordham.edu (direct PDF)

Class actions present a fundamental governance problem: a large number of individuals with small individual claims must collectively act through a representative whose interests may diverge from theirs. Amchem Products v. Windsor and Ortiz v. Fibreboard addressed this problem in the settlement class action context, requiring that the procedural requirements of Rule 23 be satisfied even when a class is certified only for purposes of settlement. The result is a judicial supervision role that courts are ill-equipped to perform.

Rule 23
Key Cases

Source 22
Robert H. Klonoff, The Decline of Class Actions
90 Wash. U. L. Rev. 729 (2013) · openscholarship.wustl.edu (direct PDF)

Beginning with the Rehnquist Court and accelerating under the Roberts Court, class action jurisprudence has moved consistently in the direction of restricting certification. The combination of Twombly/Iqbal at the pleading stage, the tightening of Rule 23(a)(2) commonality in Wal-Mart Stores v. Dukes, and the requirement of damages-model alignment at the predominance stage in Comcast v. Behrend has made class certification substantially more difficult in all but the most straightforward cases.

Source 23
Stephen B. Burbank, The Class Action Fairness Act of 2005 in Historical Context: A Preliminary View
156 U. Pa. L. Rev. 1439 (2008) · scholarship.law.upenn.edu

CAFA represented a major structural shift in the allocation of class action litigation between state and federal courts. By granting federal jurisdiction over class actions with aggregate claims exceeding five million dollars and minimal diversity, Congress federalized a substantial portion of mass tort and consumer class action litigation that had previously been litigated in state courts. The historical context makes clear that this was a deliberate policy choice designed to constrain plaintiff-friendly state court procedural rules.

Section XI · Modules 33–34

Claim & Issue Preclusion

Res judicata, collateral estoppel, non-mutual preclusion, and comparative dimensions.

Source 24
Kevin M. Clermont, Res Judicata as Requisite for Justice
68 U. Pitt. L. Rev. 301 (2006) · scholarship.law.cornell.edu (direct PDF)

Res judicata is not merely a finality rule — it is a precondition for a just judicial system. Without it, parties would be subject to serial relitigation of the same claims, courts would be overwhelmed with redundant proceedings, and the very meaning of a final judgment would be undermined. The Restatement Second of Judgments' transactional test for claim preclusion reflects a sound policy choice: all claims arising from the same transaction should be resolved in a single proceeding, or not at all.

Access to Courts & Procedure Reform

The access-to-justice implications of Twombly/Iqbal, discovery costs, and summary judgment.

Source 25
Kevin M. Clermont & Theodore Eisenberg, Do Case Outcomes Really Reveal Anything About the Legal System? Win Rates and Removal Jurisdiction
83 Cornell L. Rev. 581 (1998) · scholarship.law.cornell.edu

Empirical analysis of federal court outcomes challenges several assumptions embedded in procedural doctrine. Plaintiffs who choose to remain in state court do not systematically lose at higher rates than those in federal court, suggesting that the removal statute's forum-allocation function operates on factors other than anticipated merits. The Erie doctrine's concern with forum-shopping inequity is thus at least partially empirically grounded.

Source 26
Charles Alan Wright & Arthur R. Miller, Federal Practice and Procedure — Selected Foundational Articles
Mich. L. Rev. Review Essay (1970) · repository.law.umich.edu (direct PDF)

The Federal Rules of Civil Procedure were designed to be a unified, trans-substantive body of procedural law. The Reporter's insistence on simplicity and flexibility over technical precision was a conscious choice to trust judges and lawyers to work out the details in specific contexts. The body of doctrine that has grown up around the Rules over three decades confirms that this trust was largely well-placed, even if specific rules have generated unanticipated difficulties.

Constitutionality of Federal Procedure

Article III limits, the Rules Enabling Act, and constitutional constraints on rulemaking.

Source 27
Stephen B. Burbank, The Rules Enabling Act of 1934
130 U. Pa. L. Rev. 1015 (1982) · scholarship.law.upenn.edu

The Rules Enabling Act of 1934 delegated to the Supreme Court the power to prescribe general rules of practice and procedure for the federal courts, subject to the constraint that such rules shall not abridge, enlarge, or modify any substantive right. This constraint has proven enormously difficult to apply, as the line between substance and procedure is deeply contested. The history of the Act reveals that Congress was aware of this difficulty and deliberately left the boundary vague, entrusting its resolution to judicial judgment.

Rule 8. General Rules of Pleading

Layer: FRCP · Category: Pleading
(a) Claim for Relief. A pleading that states a claim for relief must contain: (1) a short and plain statement of the grounds for the court's jurisdiction, unless the court already has jurisdiction and the claim needs no new jurisdictional support; (2) a short and plain statement of the claim showing that the pleader is entitled to relief; and (3) a demand for the relief sought, which may include relief in the alternative or different types of relief. (b) Defenses; Admissions and Denials. (1) In General. In responding to a pleading, a party must: (A) state in short and plain terms its defenses to each claim asserted against it; and (B) admit or deny the allegations asserted against it by an opposing party. (2) Denials—Responding to the Substance. A denial must fairly respond to the substance of the allegation. (3) General and Specific Denials. A party that intends in good faith to deny all the allegations of a pleading—including the jurisdictional grounds—may do so by a general denial. A party that does not intend to deny all the allegations must either specifically deny designated allegations or generally deny all except those specifically admitted. (4) Denying Part of an Allegation. A party that intends in good faith to deny only part of an allegation must admit the part that is true and deny the rest. (5) Lacking Knowledge or Information. A party that lacks knowledge or information sufficient to form a belief about the truth of an allegation must so state, and the statement has the effect of a denial. (6) Effect of Failing to Deny. An allegation—other than one relating to the amount of damages—is admitted if a responsive pleading is required and the allegation is not denied. If a responsive pleading is not required, an allegation is considered denied or avoided. (c) Affirmative Defenses. (1) In General. In responding to a pleading, a party must affirmatively state any avoidance or affirmative defense, including: accord and satisfaction; arbitration and award; assumption of risk; contributory negligence; duress; estoppel; failure of consideration; fraud; illegality; injury by fellow servant; laches; license; payment; release; res judicata; statute of frauds; statute of limitations; and waiver. (2) Mistaken Designation. If a party mistakenly designates a defense as a counterclaim, or a counterclaim as a defense, the court must, if justice requires, treat the pleading as though it were correctly designated, and may impose terms for doing so. (d) Pleading to Be Concise and Direct; Alternative Statements; Inconsistency. (1) In General. Each allegation must be simple, concise, and direct. No technical form is required. (2) Alternative Statements of a Claim or Defense. A party may set out 2 or more statements of a claim or defense alternatively or hypothetically, either in a single count or defense or in separate ones. If a party makes alternative statements, the pleading is sufficient if any one of them is sufficient. (3) Inconsistent Claims or Defenses. A party may state as many separate claims or defenses as it has, regardless of consistency. (e) Construing Pleadings. Pleadings must be construed so as to do justice.
Notes: Notes of Advisory Committee—1937: Rule 8(a)(2) adopts the 'short and plain statement' standard drawn from code pleading. The rule was interpreted to require only 'notice pleading' under Conley v. Gibson (1957), which held a complaint should not be dismissed unless it appears beyond doubt that the plaintiff can prove no set of facts in support of his claim. Notes—2010 Amendment: 'Discharge in bankruptcy' deleted from Rule 8(c)(1) list of affirmative defenses. The pleading standard was heightened by Bell Atlantic Corp. v. Twombly (2007) and Ashcroft v. Iqbal (2009), which require that a complaint must allege 'enough facts to state a claim to relief that is plausible on its face.'

Landmark Cases (6)

Conley v. Gibson, 355 U.S. 41 (1957)

Holding: A complaint should not be dismissed for failure to state a claim unless it appears beyond doubt that the plaintiff can prove no set of facts in support of his claim.

Established the liberal 'no set of facts' standard that governed pleading for 50 years (overruled by Twombly).

Bell Atlantic Corp. v. Twombly, 550 U.S. 544 (2007)

Holding: To survive a motion to dismiss under Rule 12(b)(6), a complaint must state enough facts to state a claim to relief that is 'plausible on its face,' not merely conceivable. Retired the Conley 'no set of facts' standard.

Replaced Conley with plausibility pleading, the most significant change to pleading standards in modern procedure.

📄 Full Text (Justia) · CourtListener · Google Scholar

Ashcroft v. Iqbal, 556 U.S. 662 (2009)

Holding: The Twombly plausibility standard applies to all civil actions, not just antitrust cases. Courts should (1) identify conclusory allegations that are not entitled to the assumption of truth and (2) determine whether remaining factual allegations plausibly state a claim.

Extended Twombly's plausibility standard to all federal civil litigation and added the two-step methodology.

Leatherman v. Tarrant County Narcotics Intelligence & Coordination Unit, 507 U.S. 163 (1993)

Holding: Federal courts may not impose a heightened pleading standard in civil rights cases beyond what is required by Rules 8 and 9.

Rejected judicial adoption of heightened pleading; only Rule 9(b) requires specificity.

📄 Full Text (Justia) · Google Scholar

Swierkiewicz v. Sorema N.A., 534 U.S. 506 (2002)

Holding: An employment discrimination plaintiff need not plead specific facts establishing a prima facie case under the McDonnell Douglas framework; the ordinary Rule 8(a)(2) notice pleading standard applies.

Reinforced liberal notice pleading pre-Twombly; its vitality post-Iqbal is debated.

📄 Full Text (Justia) · Google Scholar

Parklane Hosiery Co. v. Shore, 439 U.S. 322 (1979)

Holding: Offensive nonmutual collateral estoppel may be applied at the discretion of the trial court, considering fairness factors including whether the plaintiff could easily have joined the prior action.

Approved offensive nonmutual issue preclusion, expanding the reach of prior judgments.
